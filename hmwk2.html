HOMEWORK 2

Concepts: Dataset and Distribution

This page summarizes the design, methodology, and results of the Homework 2 experiment. It includes descriptive statistics, confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and a brief interpretation of the findings.

02
OVERVIEW

Experiment Overview

The objective was to evaluate whether the treatment leads to a measurable improvement in the target metric compared to the control condition. A simple randomized design was used with two groups: Control (C) and Treatment (T).

  • Design: Two-sample comparison (independent groups)
  • Sample size: n_C = 50, n_T = 50
  • Primary metric: Continuous outcome (e.g., response time, score)
  • Significance level: \( \alpha = 0.05 \)

Data & Methods

We computed descriptive statistics, 95% confidence intervals for group means, and conducted a two-sample t-test assuming unequal variances (Welch's test). Normality was assessed visually; results were robust to mild deviations.

  • Descriptives: mean, standard deviation, standard error
  • Inference: 95% CI, Welch two-sample t-test
  • Effect size: Hedges' g with small-sample correction
03
RESULTS

Summary Statistics

Group Descriptives

The treatment group shows a lower mean (better performance) than control. The confidence intervals have limited overlap, suggesting a real difference.

Group n Mean SD 95% CI
Control 50 12.4 4.1 [11.2, 13.6]
Treatment 50 10.1 3.8 [9.0, 11.1]

Hypothesis Test

  • Null: \( \mu_T - \mu_C = 0 \)
  • Alternative: \( \mu_T - \mu_C \neq 0 \)
  • Test: Welch two-sample t-test
  • Statistic: \( t = -3.29 \)
  • df: 95.7
  • p-value: 0.0014
  • Effect size: Hedges' g = 0.60

At \( \alpha = 0.05 \), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the treatment has a statistically significant effect on the outcome.

Key Findings

  • The treatment outperforms control with a moderate effect size.
  • Results are statistically significant at the 5% level.
  • Observed variability is comparable across groups.
04
CONCLUSION

Conclusion

The analysis indicates a meaningful improvement associated with the treatment. Future work should replicate these findings with larger samples, assess robustness to distributional assumptions, and evaluate potential confounders.

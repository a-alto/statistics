Statistical Inference and Hypothesis Testing

Statistical inference is the process of drawing conclusions about populations or scientific truths from data. It provides a framework for making decisions and predictions when complete information is not available.

\[ H_0: \mu = \mu_0 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0 \]
Hypothesis testing is a fundamental tool in statistical inference. We start with a null hypothesis \( H_0 \) and an alternative hypothesis \( H_1 \). Using sample data, we calculate a test statistic and compare it to a critical value or compute a p-value to determine whether to reject the null hypothesis.
\[ \text{p-value} = P(|T| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}| \mid H_0 \text{ is true}) \]
This homework explores confidence intervals, t-tests, chi-squared tests, and other inferential procedures commonly used in data analysis and experimental design.

02

Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval provides a range of plausible values for an unknown parameter. For a population mean \( \mu \) with known variance \( \sigma^2 \), a 95% confidence interval is:

\[ \bar{x} \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]
When the variance is unknown, we use the sample standard deviation \( s \) and the t-distribution:
\[ \bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
where \( t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \) is the critical value from the t-distribution with \( n-1 \) degrees of freedom.

Example: Computing a Confidence Interval

Suppose we have a sample of \( n = 25 \) observations with sample mean \( \bar{x} = 12.5 \) and sample standard deviation \( s = 3.2 \). To compute a 95% confidence interval:

Parameter Value
Sample size (n) 25
Sample mean (\( \bar{x} \)) 12.5
Sample std dev (s) 3.2
Degrees of freedom 24
t-critical value (Î±=0.05) 2.064
Standard error 0.64
Margin of error 1.32
95% CI [11.18, 13.82]

03

Hypothesis Testing: Two-Sample t-Test

A two-sample t-test compares the means of two independent groups to determine if there is statistical evidence that the associated population means are significantly different.

\[ t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \]

Example Dataset

Suppose we have two groups of students and we want to compare their test scores:

Group Sample Size Mean Std Dev
Group A (Control) 30 75.4 8.2
Group B (Treatment) 30 79.8 7.6

Test Results

  • Null Hypothesis: \( H_0: \mu_A = \mu_B \)
  • Alternative Hypothesis: \( H_1: \mu_A \neq \mu_B \)
  • Test Statistic: \( t = -2.15 \)
  • Degrees of Freedom: 58
  • p-value: 0.036
  • Conclusion: At \( \alpha = 0.05 \), we reject \( H_0 \) and conclude that there is a significant difference between the two groups.

04

Chi-Squared Test of Independence

The chi-squared test of independence determines whether there is a significant association between two categorical variables. The test statistic is:

\[ \chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sum_{j=1}^{c} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}} \]
where \( O_{ij} \) is the observed frequency and \( E_{ij} \) is the expected frequency under the null hypothesis of independence.

Example: Gender and Major Preference

We want to test whether there is an association between gender and choice of major among students.

Gender / Major STEM Humanities Business Total
Male 45 20 35 100
Female 30 40 30 100
Total 75 60 65 200

Test Results

  • Chi-squared statistic: \( \chi^2 = 12.82 \)
  • Degrees of freedom: 2
  • p-value: 0.0016
  • Conclusion: At \( \alpha = 0.05 \), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a significant association between gender and major choice.

05

ANOVA: Analysis of Variance

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is used to compare means across three or more groups. The F-statistic tests whether at least one group mean is significantly different from the others:

\[ F = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{between}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{within}}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{between}}/(k-1)}{\text{SS}_{\text{within}}/(N-k)} \]
where \( k \) is the number of groups and \( N \) is the total sample size.

Example: Comparing Three Teaching Methods

We want to compare the effectiveness of three different teaching methods on student performance.

Source Sum of Squares df Mean Square F p-value
Between Groups 320.5 2 160.25 8.42 0.0004
Within Groups 1140.2 60 19.00 - -
Total 1460.7 62 - - -

Conclusion

With \( F = 8.42 \) and \( p = 0.0004 < 0.05 \), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there are significant differences in mean performance across the three teaching methods. Post-hoc tests (e.g., Tukey's HSD) would be needed to determine which specific pairs of methods differ.
